CLINICAL REFERENCE: Differential Diagnosis Guide

RESPIRATORY SYMPTOMS DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS
=============================================

CHIEF COMPLAINT: Fever + Cough + Dyspnea (Acute)
--------------------------------------------------
Primary considerations:
1. Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP) — ICD-10: J18.9
   Key features: Productive cough, fever > 38°C, lobar consolidation on CXR, WBC elevated
   
2. Acute bronchitis — ICD-10: J20.9
   Key features: Cough (with or without sputum), usually afebrile or low-grade fever
   Labs: WBC normal or mildly elevated, CXR normal
   
3. Viral upper respiratory tract infection (URI) — ICD-10: J06.9
   Key features: Rhinorrhea, sore throat, myalgia, usually low-grade fever
   Labs: WBC normal or low with lymphocytosis
   
4. Influenza — ICD-10: J11.1
   Key features: Abrupt onset, high fever, myalgias, headache, dry cough
   Labs: WBC normal or leukopenia, rapid flu test
   
5. COVID-19 pneumonia — ICD-10: U07.1
   Key features: Fever, cough, loss of taste/smell, bilateral infiltrates
   Labs: Lymphopenia, elevated D-dimer, elevated CRP
   
6. Pulmonary embolism (PE) — ICD-10: I26.9
   Key features: Pleuritic chest pain, dyspnea, tachycardia, recent immobility
   Labs: Elevated D-dimer, ABG showing hypoxemia, CXR may show Hampton's hump
   
7. Congestive heart failure (CHF) with pulmonary edema — ICD-10: I50.9
   Key features: Orthopnea, bilateral crackles, elevated JVP, edema
   Labs: Elevated BNP/NT-proBNP, CXR showing bilateral infiltrates + cardiomegaly
   
8. COPD exacerbation — ICD-10: J44.1
   Key features: Known COPD history, increased dyspnea, change in sputum
   
9. Lung abscess — ICD-10: J85.2
   Key features: Foul-smelling sputum, prolonged fever, risk factors (aspiration, dental disease)
   
10. Tuberculosis (TB) — ICD-10: A15.0
    Key features: Night sweats, weight loss, hemoptysis, upper lobe cavitation, exposure history

DIFFERENTIATING BACTERIAL vs VIRAL PNEUMONIA:
Bacterial (more likely):
- Sudden onset, high fever > 39°C
- Productive purulent sputum
- WBC > 15.0 with neutrophilia
- CRP > 100 mg/L
- Lobar consolidation on CXR
- Responds to antibiotics in 24-48 hours

Viral (more likely):
- Gradual onset, myalgias, malaise
- Dry or scant cough
- WBC normal or leukopenia
- Bilateral interstitial infiltrates on CXR
- Does not respond to antibiotics
- May have lymphocytosis

CHEST PAIN DIFFERENTIAL
========================
1. Pleuritis — ICD-10: R07.1 (pleuritic pain, worse with inspiration)
2. Musculoskeletal — ICD-10: M79.3 (reproducible with palpation)
3. Pericarditis — ICD-10: I30.9 (positional, pleuritic, friction rub)
4. Acute coronary syndrome (ACS) — ICD-10: I21.9 (pressure-like, radiation)
5. Aortic dissection — ICD-10: I71.0 (tearing, maximal at onset, back radiation)
6. Pulmonary embolism — ICD-10: I26.9 (pleuritic + dyspnea + risk factors)
7. GERD/esophageal — ICD-10: K21.0 (burning, postprandial, improves with antacids)

COMMON CLINICAL PATTERNS
=========================

SEPSIS PATTERN:
Diagnosis: Infection + 2+ SIRS criteria
- Temperature > 38°C or < 36°C
- HR > 90 bpm
- RR > 20 or PaCO2 < 32 mmHg
- WBC > 12.0 or < 4.0 or > 10% bands
ICD-10: A41.9 (sepsis, unspecified organism)
Must order: Blood cultures x2, lactate, CBC, BMP, LFTs, coagulation studies

SIRS without infection:
- Pancreatitis, burn injury, trauma, post-surgical

URINARY TRACT INFECTION (UTI) PATTERN:
- Dysuria, frequency, urgency
- UA: pyuria (> 5 WBCs/hpf), bacteriuria, nitrites positive
- Fever + flank pain: suggests pyelonephritis (ICD-10: N10)
- First-line treatment: TMP-SMX or nitrofurantoin (uncomplicated UTI)
  Fluoroquinolones: reserved for complicated UTI, pyelonephritis

TYPICAL PATIENT PRESENTATIONS:
================================

Presentation: 54-year-old male with fever, productive cough x 3 days, WBC 18.4
Most likely diagnosis: Bacterial pneumonia (CAP) — ICD-10: J18.9
Supporting: Age, male sex, WBC elevation, productive cough, fever
Action: CXR, blood cultures, start empirical antibiotics

Presentation: 70-year-old female with altered mental status, fever, increased respiratory rate
Consider: CAP with sepsis (ICD-10: J18.9 + A41.9), UTI with sepsis, meningitis
Note: In elderly, pneumonia may present as confusion without classic respiratory symptoms

Presentation: Young adult with gradual onset bilateral infiltrates, mild fever, "walking pneumonia"
Most likely: Atypical pneumonia — ICD-10: J15.7 (Mycoplasma pneumoniae)
Treatment: Azithromycin (macrolide covers Mycoplasma); beta-lactams do NOT cover Mycoplasma

Presentation: COPD patient with worsening dyspnea, increased purulent sputum
Most likely: COPD exacerbation (ICD-10: J44.1) with or without pneumonia
Additional organisms: H. influenzae, Moraxella catarrhalis (common in COPD)

RISK STRATIFICATION TOOLS:
===========================

PSI (Pneumonia Severity Index) / PORT Score:
Class I (age < 50, no comorbidities, normal vitals): Outpatient
Class II-III (score < 70-90): Outpatient or brief observation
Class IV-V (score 91-130, > 130): Hospitalization required

CURB-65 (simpler tool):
Score 0-1: Outpatient treatment
Score 2: Short hospitalization or supervised outpatient
Score 3-5: Hospitalization (score 4-5: consider ICU)

Fine criteria for ICU admission:
- Respiratory rate > 30 breaths/min
- PaO2/FiO2 ratio < 250
- Multilobar infiltrates
- Confusion/disorientation
- BUN > 20 mg/dL
- WBC < 4.0 x10^3/uL
- Platelet < 100
- Core temperature < 36°C
- Hypotension requiring IV fluids
Minor criteria — 3+ = ICU; Major criteria: 1 = ICU
